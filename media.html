NeckSense

NeckSense is a wearable sensing device that is able to collect features associated with eating and drinking. These features include bites, chews, lean-forward angle, and ambient light. NeckSense is currently being used to collect free-living data in conjunction with 2 additional wearable sensing devices, each of which collect additional behavioral features.NeckSense's primary measure is taken by a proximity sensor that records the distance between the top of the device and the bottom of the wearer's chin. Necksense is able to identify chews in real-time by applying an onboard machine learning model to proxmity sensor's output. The model benefits from distinguishing physical characteristics of chewing, namely the consistency of amplitude and frequency exhibited by the wave-shape produced when mapping proximity data over time.

VibroScale

VibroScale is a mobile application that allows users to weigh objects with their phones. Users open VibroScale, lay their phone down on a flat surface, and press a button in the VibroScale app. This causes the phone to vibrate. VibroScale records the output of the phone's inertial measurement unit (IMU) to obtain baseline readings of phone's movement (accelerometer) and orientation (gyroscope) throughout the vibration. Users then place the object they would like to weigh onto their phone and initiate a second vibration. VibroScale records IMU output during the second vibration and compares it to the IMU output from the first vibration.

Differences in the two IMU readings are mapped to the weight of the object. The estimation is performed by a regression algorithm trained on IMU readings of phone vibrations taken before and after placing objects of known weight onto the phone. Training the algorithm this way allows VibroScale to remain accurate despite differences in the attributes of weighed objects that perturb the patterns of IMU activity collected during vibration. For example, consider an apple and a potato with identical weights. The differing shapes and textures of these foods produce different IMU output during vibration. The VibroScale regression algorithm, trained on food items of varying type, is capable of estimating weight without relying on an assumption of the object's physical attributes.
