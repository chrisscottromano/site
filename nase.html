Amplification of Negative Experience Suggests Dissociation of Sensation and Enjoyment

Romano, CS

Feinberg School of Medicine,
Northwestern University




Abstract

According to Amplification Theory, pleasant experiences are judged more positively when shared, and unpleasant experiences are judged more negatively when shared. Investigations of this effect with respect to the relationship between co-experiencers show psychological distance to moderate the amplification of positive experience — one must feel close to their co-experiencer for the experience in question to be amplified. However, amplification has not previously been shown in conditions of negative experience, and the implications of such an effect would seemingly conflict with principles of operant conditioning. The present research aims to examine the effect of high psychological proximity on the judgment of a shared negative experience. The results support Amplification Theory’s key predictions, but also reveal a flaw in how experience is conceptualized in the amplification literature. Findings are discussed with respect to prevailing explanations of the Amplification Effect and future study of Amplification Theory is advised to adopt a clarified notion of experience that resolves the apparent conflict between negative amplification and operant conditioning.

Keywords: amplification, negative experience, psychological distance

Amplification

Sensory experience informs judgments that are influenced by the context in which they are formed. Aspects of context may be internally generated, as is the case with mood states (Palermo 2017; Clark & Isen 1982). External influences of judgment include those that extend from the presence of others. For example, normative social influence can coerce an individual’s stated evaluation of certain sensory input (Asch 1955). However, this type of judgmental shift is somewhat disingenuous and may better represent the concealing of judgments, not their direct augmentation. To examine social influence as it affects the uncoerced judgment of sensory stimuli, we must manipulate social preconditions of sensation, rather than those of the judgment-reporting process.

Recent experimental findings suggest that sensory experience, whether positive or negative, will be judged as more intense if the experience is shared with another person (Boothby, Clark, & Bargh 2014). This is to say that evaluating an experience in concert with another person may skew perception and judgment of said experience toward its respective extreme. The correspondence of this judgmental shift’s direction to the valence of the experience led the authors to term this process the ‘Amplification Effect’. However, the heart of the matter remains untouched until we consider the nature and influence of co-experiencer relationships.

 

Time, Space, and Friendship

Amplification is absent when co-experiencers do not share an in-group (Shteynberg & Apfelbaum, 2013; Shteynberg, Hirsh, Galinsky, & Knight, 2014). Amplification is also absent when co-experiencers do not share a location (Boothby, Clark & Bargh, 2014). Furthermore, experience is essentially unshared if co-experiencers engage with the experience at different times. From this, we gather that high spatial, temporal, and psychological proximity all seem to be necessary conditions for amplification to occur. As of now, the bulk of studies in this area concern amplification of moods and learning processes. Amplification of sensory experience in regard to spatial, temporal, and psychological distance has only been observed in conditions of positive experience.

 

Attentional-Resource Account

Amplification is thought to occur as a result of heightened attention to a given experience (Shteynberg, 2010; Shteynberg, Hirsh, Apfelbaum, et al., 2014). This is supported by research demonstrating the tendency to direct attention to the object of another person’s attention (e.g., Friesen & Kingstone, 1998; Friesen, Moore, & Kingstone, 2005; Langton & Bruce, 1999; Samson, Apperly, Braithwaite, Andrews, & Bodley Scott, 2010). Construal-Level Theory’s concept of mental travel may apply, as no significant mental travel is necessary when all dimensions of abstraction are highly proximal (Trope & Liberman, 2010). It stands to reason that the resulting non-necessity of mental travel frees up attentional resources within the cognitive economy. This surplus of attentional capability is then utilized in attending to a shared experience, thus intensifying the perceptual input.

The theoretical framework of this account predicts that an optimized cognitive economy, directed in part by the attention schema of one’s co-experiencer, will produce perceptual accentuation, or amplification, of the sensory content of a shared experience. This perspective provides us with a coherent account of a process that pushes a given sensory judgment toward its diametric extreme, rather than imposing a directionally uniform effect upon all such judgments.

 

Negative Amplification

While the cognitive-economical model of amplification is logically consistent and has received empirical support, it seems to conflict with the principles of operant conditioning when considered against human tendencies (Skinner, 1938). If sharing a negative experience with a psychologically close person increases the unpleasantness of said experience, why has the practice not been conditioned out of the human behavioral inventory? Why do we not prefer to share our negative experiences with strangers, rather than friends and family? Returning to the emotional domain, why do we commiserate with our families instead of fellow train passengers? If the amplification effect truly worsens negative experience, why have we not seen our tendencies adapt to avoid such moments? It seems that either our decision to share negative experiences with those closest to us is irrational, or that psychological closeness operates differently in its moderation of positive vs. negative amplification.

The present research aims to examine the latter possibility. The study maintains the procedures employed by Boothby et al., save for the author’s replacement of pleasant tasting chocolate with unpleasant tasting chocolate (2016). It is the objective of this study to examine amplification of negative shared experience under conditions of high spatial, temporal, and psychological proximity.

 

Methods

Participants. Participants were 38 students recruited from introductory psychology classes at Loyola University of Chicago. The study was advertised under the purposely vague title of “Sensory Experience Study.” All participants were compensated with course credit.

 

Procedure. Participants arrived at the laboratory and waited in the lobby. A confederate, posing as a second participant, waited in the lobby with the real participant. The experimenter called the name of the participant and the false name of the confederate before escorting both the participant and the confederate to the observation lab. Informed consent was then administered.

 

Negative Experience. The chocolate used in the experiment had a cacao content of 92%. The flavor of the chocolate was bitter to say the least. Eating the chocolate was pretested by colleagues and confirmed to be a negative experience.

 

Psychological Distance. Participants assigned to the psychologically proximal conditions would perform a modified relationship closeness induction task with the confederate. The experimenter introduced the task as a way for participants to get to know each other before the experiment began.  This task was adapted from the Boothby et al. (2016) adaptation of the Relationship Closeness Induction Inventory designed by Campbell, Reader, and Elliot (1999). The participant and confederate were given a packet containing three lists of increasingly personal questions (Appendix A). The group was instructed to take turns asking each other the questions for 10 minutes or until they completed the entire packet. The confederate’s answers were memorized and scripted to be relatively generic (Appendix B).  Participants assigned to psychologically distant conditions would not perform the relationship closeness induction task. The group effectively remained strangers in these conditions.

 

Shared / Unshared Experience. Immediately after the manipulation of psychological distance, participants were told they would be rating various sensory experiences. Specifically, they were told they would be tasting chocolate and viewing art, the latter of which would never actually happen. In the shared experience condition, the participant and confederate were both given a square of chocolate and a response questionnaire on which they were to rate the chocolate as well as their feelings towards the other participant. The participant and confederate were instructed to refrain from interacting with each other during this procedure. In the unshared experience condition, the experimenter would tell the confederate they would be viewing art while the participant tasted the chocolate. In this exchange the experimenter would refer to the confederate as “participant B” to imply a behind-the-scenes random assignment. The confederate would then leave the room and the participant would be given the chocolate and response questionnaire. The participants were then instructed to taste the chocolate and respond to the questions alone. After the surveys were collected, the trial was terminated, and the participant was debriefed.

 

Measures

Chocolate Rating. Participants answered several questions about the taste of the chocolate as well as the experience of eating the chocolate (Appendix C). Taste responses were aggregated into the composite item chocolate enjoyment, while experience responses were aggregated into the composite item experience enjoyment.

 

Interpersonal Closeness. Participants answered several questions regarding the confederate (whom they assumed to be another participant) (Appendix D). Closeness responses were aggregated into the composite item interpersonal closeness. All items in both measures were rated on 11-point scales ranging from 0 (least close) to 10 (closest).

 

Results

A single participant rated the chocolate’s flavor favorably. This participant did not have a negative sensory experience and, as such, was excluded from analysis.


 

Chocolate enjoyment ratings were subjected to a two-way analysis of variance in which a significant effect of the psychological-distance + shared-experience interaction was found, F (1, 34) = 4.61, p = 0.039. Post-hoc analysis revealed this effect to be significant between the acquainted + shared and acquainted + unshared conditions, t (34) = -2.52, p = 0.017. Independent samples t-tests were administered to compare mean differences in chocolate ratings between each condition. Participants in the acquainted + shared condition assigned significantly lower enjoyment of the chocolate (M = 0.89, SD = 0.86) than did those in the acquainted + unshared condition (M = 2.80, SD = 2.12), t (17) = -2.52, p = 0.022. That is, acquainted participants reported liking the chocolate less in the shared experience condition than in the unshared experience condition (see fig. 1).

No significant difference was found in experience enjoyment between the acquainted + shared (M = 3.94, SD = 2.63) and acquainted + unshared conditions (M = 4.45, SD = 2.93), t (17) = -0.39, p = 0.70. While acquainted participants differed in their enjoyment of the chocolate, their enjoyment of the overall experience was unaffected (see fig.2).

 

In unacquainted groups, no significant difference was found in chocolate ratings between shared and unshared conditions. Participants in the unacquainted + shared condition gave chocolate enjoyment ratings (M = 1.94, SD = 1.74) similar to those of participants in the unacquainted + unshared condition (M = 1.55, SD = 1.57), t (17) = 0.52, p = 0.61. Differences in experience enjoyment between participants in the unacquainted + shared condition (M = 4.61, SD = 2.32) and participants in the unacquainted + unshared condition (M = 2.60, SD = 2.02) approached significance (that is, unacquainted + unshared participants averaged reporting 2 points lower than unacquainted + shared participants) but did not quite reach it (see fig. 2), t (17) = 2.02, p = 0.059.

 

Measurements of interpersonal closeness were also subjected to independent samples t-test analysis. Participants in the acquainted + shared condition (M = 6.74, SD = 1.66) did not vary significantly from those in the acquainted + unshared condition (M = 5.57, SD = 1.29) in their reported levels of interpersonal closeness to the confederate, t (17) = 1.736, p = 0.101. However, a significant difference did emerge between the two unacquainted conditions. Participants in the unacquainted + unshared condition reported much lower feelings of interpersonal closeness (M = 2.55, SD = 1.78) than participants in the unacquainted + shared condition (M = 5.07, SD = 1.97), t (17) = 2.93, p < 0.009. While neither group of participants became acquainted with the confederate, those who shared the chocolate eating experience with the confederate reported scores consistent with the acquainted conditions (acquainted + shared used for reference), t (16) = 1.951, p = 0.069. Analyses of variance between these conditions was forgone as it revealed the same information as the aforementioned t-tests in a less coherent manner.

 

 

Discussion

General

The results and implications of this study are less straightforward than those produced by Boothby, Smith, Clark, and Bargh under conditions of positive sensory experience (2016). The central claims of Amplification Theory are supported by the evidence. Reducing spatial, temporal, and psychological distances to nearly zero did, in fact, result in amplification of the negative experience (just as it would a positive experience). However, this amplification was limited to the enjoyment of the chocolate itself and did not have any measurable effect on overall experience. This suggests, at least in conditions of unpleasant sensory stimulation, an independent judgment of sensory experience that is evaluatively distinct from its superordinate structure of total experience.

Most, if not all of the literature in this domain seems to quietly accept that these two types of experience react to circumstance in concert. The results of the present research indicate that this is not so, highlighting a need for closer examination of the differences between pure sensory experience and overall experience.

An unexpected interaction appeared in the interpersonal closeness ratings. Participants in the shared + unacquainted condition reported interpersonal closeness scores comparable to those of participants in acquainted conditions. It appears sharing an unpleasant experience with a stranger has an acquainting effect of similar magnitude to that of the relationship-closeness induction task. The shared + unacquainted group’s chocolate enjoyment ratings did not exhibit amplification, indicating that this relationship-closeness induction did not occur until participants were asked to rate their perceived closeness to the confederate. Thus, it appears the interpersonal closeness judgment reports were influenced in similar manner to the sensory judgment reports in Asch (1955). That is, the reported interpersonal closeness was coerced after the fact, only shifting when the judgment was requested by the survey measure rather than having an immediate onset following the experience.

Also worth discussion is the nearly significant difference between overall experience ratings in the two unacquainted conditions. Given the sample size, it seems reasonable to entertain the notion that further study may show this difference to indeed be significant. If so, a new line of inquiry may be necessary to understand how, with no difference in perceived flavor, a person will enjoy the total experience of tasting bad chocolate with a stranger more than if the same chocolate had been tasted alone. It would seem that this potential finding, if confirmed by future study, constitutes a reversal of the Amplification Effect in which total experience is evaluatively augmented both independently of sensory experience and in a valence direction opposite to the direction anticipated by the theory of amplification.

While this potential finding may be proven to be no more than conjecture, it is lent theoretical credibility by the aforementioned dissociation between chocolate enjoyment (sensory) and experience enjoyment (total).

 

Evidence for De-Amplification

Another finding of note is the trend towards de-amplification that is found in the data. The acquainted + unshared participant group (rated the chocolate most favorably) did not report their chocolate enjoyment as significantly higher than groups in either of the unacquainted conditions. Neither did the acquainted + shared participant group (rated the chocolate least favorably) report their chocolate enjoyment as significantly lower than groups in either of the unacquainted conditions. With unacquainted participant groups showing no quantitative allegiance to either extreme, we may tentatively consider them ‘in the middle’, just as they were in studies of shared positive sensory experience. Such an arrangement of outcomes supports de-amplification as it is postulated by Boothby, Smith, Clark, and Bargh (2016). This postulate asserts that if a person becomes psychologically proximate to a second person, who then immediately leaves to do something else, the first person will allocate attentional resources towards the imagined activities of the second person. This tax on the cognitive economy lessens the impact of any sensory stimulation subsequently imposed upon the first person. In fact, the distractive quality of the second person is of sufficient magnitude that the first person’s sensory experience is dampened, or de-amplified, past the point that it would have been had s/he simply engaged in the sensory experience alone.

           

Attentional Resource Perspective - revisited

The attentional resource perspective offers a thorough, easy to understand, and seemingly valid approach to amplification. While it cannot explicitly account for the dissociation between sensory and total experience, it is not necessarily under any obligation to do so.

 

Evidence of Experiential Double Dissociation

The finding of principal interest to the present study is the apparent double-dissociation of sensory and total experience. While the two are necessarily distinct, amplification literature presupposes them as acting and reacting in tandem to amplification. The present study constructed scenarios in which chocolate enjoyment (operation of sensory experience) and experience enjoyment (operation of total experience) showed variations independent of the other. With this we can discard a concern mentioned earlier in this article: that negative amplification may violate principles of operant conditioning. Commiserative behaviors do not seek a sensory experience, but rather a total experience. Thus, the more consistently total experience is demonstrated to be detachable from sensory experience in certain sharing conditions, the less we need to concern ourselves about operant conditioning. In fact, it may even be reasonable to hypothesize that a sensitive commiserative situation, if evaluated exclusively in terms of its sensory content, could possibly be subject to negative amplification if the commiserate agent is psychologically proximate to the miserable.

 

Future Directions

In conclusion, I propose a direction for the future study of amplification. The 3 dimensions of distance (spatial, temporal, psychological) should be considered as being of fundamental importance to amplification. Manipulation of these distances to near-zero levels returned consistent results in the outcomes of primary importance to the theory. The agreements between construal-level theory, cognitive economics, and the attentional resource account of amplification lend strong credibility to this proposal.

Further, it is imperative that more insight is gleaned into the relationship between sensory and total experience. Given our largely incomplete understanding of the dynamics at these levels of organization, further study of amplification should commit the theory to being one of sensory experience, rather than one of total experience.

In pursuing this direction, we are able to cleanse our research of a major conflation while also extending our theoretical model into one which offers a broader scope of possibility in the effort to understand how judgments are constructed from perceptual experience, and how social influence plays a part at both the perceptual and judgmental stages of this process.
